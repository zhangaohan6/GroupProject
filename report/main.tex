\title{Fine-Grained Species Classification on iNaturalist-2021:\\
Traditional and Deep Pipelines with a Robustness and Explainability Study}

\begin{abstract}
We study fine-grained visual classification on a 500-class subset of
iNaturalist-2021 (40 train / 10 test images per class, fixed seed~42). We compare a
traditional Bag-of-Visual-Words (BoVW) SIFT + linear-SVM pipeline against
convolutional networks trained from scratch and fine-tuned from ImageNet-pretrained
weights. The handcrafted pipeline reaches only \textbf{2.9\%} top-1 (macro-F1 0.02),
which confirms that local-descriptor histograms cannot separate 500 visually similar
species. An ImageNet-pretrained ResNet-50 fine-tuned for 12 epochs reaches
\textbf{68.4\%} top-1, \textbf{86.1\%} top-5 and \textbf{0.684} macro-F1. The same
architecture trained from scratch reaches only \textbf{25.8\%} top-1 (macro-F1 0.245),
so pretraining alone accounts for a $+42.6$-point top-1 gap. We then run
two advanced studies on the fixed pretrained model. First, a test-time degradation
robustness sweep over five corruption types and five severities shows the model
is most fragile to blur (Gaussian-blur top-1 falls from 0.68 to 0.08 by severity~3)
and most tolerant to JPEG compression. Second, a Grad-CAM analysis shows correct
predictions localise on the organism, whereas errors attend to background context.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
% [rubric 1pt]
Automated species recognition supports biodiversity monitoring, citizen-science
platforms and ecological surveying at a scale that expert labelling cannot reach.
iNaturalist-2021 is a standard benchmark for the harder version of this problem,
\emph{fine-grained} classification, where classes are individual species with high
intra-class variation (pose, lighting, life stage, background) and low inter-class
variation between near-identical congeners, on top of a heavy long tail. We restrict the
graded evaluation to a fixed random 500-class subset (seed 42) with a balanced
40/10 train/test split drawn from the official \texttt{train\_mini} and \texttt{val}
splits, and we report the exact class list and counts so the split can be reproduced.
Our contributions are: (1) a like-for-like comparison of a classical BoVW-SIFT+SVM
pipeline against from-scratch and ImageNet-pretrained ResNet-50 under an identical
data protocol; (2) controlled ablations that isolate transfer, architecture and
augmentation; and (3) two advanced studies on the fixed best model, namely a
test-time corruption-robustness sweep and a Grad-CAM explainability analysis.

\section{Literature Review}\label{sec:lit}
% [rubric 2pt]
Classical pipelines describe images with local invariant descriptors (SIFT, HOG, LBP)
quantised into a visual vocabulary (Bag-of-Visual-Words) and classified with an SVM or
random forest. These excel on rigid-object and texture recognition but struggle on
fine-grained natural imagery, where discriminative cues are subtle and spatially
localised. Deep CNNs replaced handcrafted features with end-to-end learned
representations; on small per-class budgets, \emph{transfer learning} from ImageNet
dominates from-scratch training. Fine-grained recognition adds part/attention
mechanisms and second-order (bilinear) pooling. For interpretability we use
Grad-CAM~\cite{gradcam}, and for corruption robustness we follow the common-corruption
protocol of Hendrycks and Dietterich~\cite{hendrycks}. Dataset: \cite{inat2021}.

\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}
% [rubric 4pt]
\subsection{Traditional pipeline: BoVW-SIFT + SVM}
Dense/keypoint SIFT descriptors are extracted per image, a $k{=}512$-word codebook is
learned with mini-batch $k$-means over the training descriptors, each image is encoded
as an $\ell_2$-normalised 512-bin visual-word histogram, and a linear SVM
(SGD-trained hinge loss, one-vs-rest over 500 classes) is fit on the histograms.
We use the SGD formulation rather than a batch \texttt{LinearSVC} because the latter
does not scale to a 500-class one-vs-rest problem in acceptable time.

\subsection{Deep pipeline: CNN (scratch vs.\ pretrained)}
Both deep models are ResNet-50 at $224{\times}224$. The \emph{pretrained} variant
initialises from ImageNet weights and fine-tunes all layers (12 epochs, Adam,
lr~$10^{-3}$, batch~64) with standard augmentation (random resized crop, horizontal
flip, colour jitter). The \emph{from-scratch} variant is identical in architecture,
augmentation and optimiser but starts from random initialisation and trains for more
epochs, isolating the effect of transferred features under a matched protocol.

\section{Experimental Results}\label{sec:exp}
% [rubric 4pt]
\subsection{Setup and metrics}
Fixed 500-class subset, 40 train / 10 test per class, seed~42; no class overlap and no
data leakage between splits. We report top-1, top-5, overall and balanced accuracy, and
macro precision/recall/F1, emphasising \textbf{macro-F1} because the task is balanced
across 500 classes and macro-F1 penalises collapse onto easy classes. We also report
train/test wall-clock time on Apple-silicon MPS.

\subsection{Main comparison}
Table~\ref{tab:main} summarises the headline comparison. Transfer learning improves
macro-F1 by roughly $23\times$ over the handcrafted baseline. We report the from-scratch
run so that we can separate how much of that gap comes from the architecture and how
much comes from the transferred ImageNet features.

\subsection{Ablations}
We vary one factor at a time: (a) transfer (scratch vs.\ pretrained, Table~\ref{tab:main});
(b) augmentation on/off; (c) codebook size $k$ for BoVW. The pretrained training curves
(Fig.~\ref{fig:curves}) show a wide train/val gap: train accuracy saturates near
1.0 while validation plateaus at 0.68 by epoch~8. We initially expected the scratch
model to keep closing this gap given more epochs, but its validation accuracy flattened
around 0.27 by epoch~19 (Fig.~\ref{fig:curves}), which suggests the 40-image/class budget
is the real bottleneck and that transfer, not capacity, drives the result. The 500-class
confusion matrix and the hardest confused species pairs (Fig.~\ref{fig:conf}) put most of
the off-diagonal mass on visually near-identical congeners.

\emph{Augmentation.} Retraining the pretrained ResNet-50 with augmentation disabled but
everything else fixed drops test top-1 from 0.684 to 0.651 and macro-F1 from 0.684 to
0.651 (Table~\ref{tab:abl}). Without augmentation the model reaches near-perfect training
accuracy even faster, so the roughly 3-point drop is the augmentation's regularisation
value under the small per-class budget.

\emph{Codebook size.} For the BoVW pipeline we sweep the visual vocabulary size $k$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:codebook}). Accuracy is essentially flat from $k{=}128$ to $512$ and
improves only slightly at $k{=}1024$ (top-1 0.029 to 0.035). A larger codebook helps a
little but cannot lift the handcrafted pipeline off its low ceiling, which is consistent
with SIFT histograms simply lacking the discriminative power for 500 fine-grained classes.

\subsection{Advanced study I: Test-time degradation robustness}\label{sec:robust}
Keeping the pretrained model \emph{fixed} (no retraining), we degrade \emph{only} the
test set with five corruption families at five severities each
(Table~\ref{tab:robust}). Clean top-1 is 0.684. The model is most fragile to
\textbf{Gaussian blur} and \textbf{motion blur}. Blur removes the fine texture cues that
fine-grained recognition depends on, so top-1 drops below 0.10 by severity~3. It is far
more tolerant to \textbf{JPEG} and mild \textbf{brightness} shifts, which preserve
global shape. The motion-blur result matters in practice, since field and phone imagery
is frequently motion-blurred.

\subsection{Advanced study II: Grad-CAM explainability}\label{sec:cam}
Figure~\ref{fig:gradcam} shows Grad-CAM saliency for the fixed pretrained model.
On correctly classified images the activation concentrates on the organism (body,
distinctive markings). On errors it tends to drift to background context such as foliage
or water, and on confusable pairs the map highlights the shared coarse silhouette rather
than the discriminating part. This lines up with the blur results above: the model leans
on localised high-frequency organism detail, which is exactly what blur removes.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:disc}
% [rubric 1pt]
The pretrained CNN wins because ImageNet features already encode the mid-level texture
and shape primitives that separate species. With only 40 images per class the scratch
model cannot learn these primitives, and a fixed SIFT vocabulary cannot represent them
at all. Failure cases cluster on near-identical congeners and on images where the organism
is small relative to the background. Taken together, the robustness sweep and the Grad-CAM
maps point at the same thing: the model's discriminative signal is localised organism
texture, so it is accurate on clean images but brittle under blur.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conc}
% [rubric 1pt]
Transfer learning is the deciding factor for 500-class fine-grained recognition. The
pretrained ResNet-50 reaches 68.4\% top-1, against 25.8\% for the identical architecture
trained from scratch and 2.9\% for BoVW. Because architecture, augmentation and optimiser
are matched between the two deep runs, the $+42.6$-point top-1 gap is attributable to the
ImageNet pretraining alone. The resulting model is still fragile to blur and depends on
background on the hard cases. For future work we would try fine-grained part attention,
blur-augmented training, and long-tail rebalancing.
